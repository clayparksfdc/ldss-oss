1c:T406f,<h2>Data for Grounding</h2>
<p>Data provides the LLM with relevant, specific, and timely information from your Salesforce environment or external sources. This enables it to generate accurate, personalized, and contextually appropriate outputs that go beyond its general training data. This bridges the gap between the LLM's broad knowledge and the specific realities of your Salesforce world. Data for grounding includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>CRM data</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>External data such as web search, user input, call transcript, or inbound email
While LLMs are incredibly knowledgeable, they don't inherently know <em>your</em> customers, <em>your</em> processes, or <em>your</em> data within Salesforce. Grounding is essential because it provides:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Relevance</strong>. Without grounding, the LLM often produces generic outputs that don't quite fit your specific Salesforce context.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Personalization.</strong> Providing specific details like customer names, account history, or case information enables the LLM to tailor its responses to feel genuinely relevant and impactful.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Accuracy.</strong> When the LLM uses your verified Salesforce data, the output is more likely to be accurate and reliable for your specific business needs. Relying solely on the LLM's general knowledge often leads to inaccuracies or hallucinations.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Context.</strong> Grounding provides the essential context the LLM needs to understand the nuances of your request. For example, knowing the stage of an Opportunity or the priority of a Case helps the LLM generate more helpful and targeted responses.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h4>Best Practices</h4>
<p>Be efficient with your data. Carefully select data that is directly pertinent to the task at hand. Avoid feeding the LLM irrelevant information because it causes confusion and leads to less effective outputs.</p>
<ul>
<li>Think about which Salesforce objects, like Accounts, Contacts, Opportunities, and Cases, and fields contain the information the LLM needs to complete the task effectively.</li>
<li>Remember that the amount of data you add to a prompt is included in the overall token count, which potentially affects the cost of using the LLM.</li>
<li>Larger amounts of data tend to also increase the processing time for the LLM.
The more specific and high quality your data is, the more specific and high quality the output is. For use cases where recency matters, like summarizing recent sales performance or open support tickets, provide the most up-to-date information available. If your data is outdated, inaccurate, or incomplete, the LLM output often reflects those flaws. The concept of garbage in, garbage out applies here.</li>
</ul>
<p>The LLM needs to be able to easily access and understand the data you provide. Use clear and structured formats, whether it's through Salesforce merge fields or well-organized text blocks.</p>
<ul>
<li>If the data has specific attributes or nuances that aren’t immediately obvious, briefly describe them to the LLM beforehand.</li>
<li>For complex data structures, providing the LLM with a clear example of the data and the desired output often significantly improves its understanding and the accuracy of its response.
Include specific field-level data, like names, roles, and company names, to make the LLM's output feel more tailored and personalized to the individuals involved. Use Salesforce merge syntax, <code>{!object.field}</code>, to dynamically pull this information.</li>
</ul>
<p>Explore RAG for information that lives outside of your immediate Salesforce records, like knowledge base articles, internal documents, or even external websites.</p>

<h3>External Knowledge with RAG</h3>
<p>Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is a natural language processing technique that combines retrieval-based and generative models. RAG provides an LLM domain-specific knowledge retrieved from sources <em>outside</em> of its original training data and the immediate prompt context. Through <strong>Data Cloud,</strong> companies provide access to their most relevant and current proprietary data to make LLM-generated responses more accurate and tailored to their exact needs.</p>
<p>Use RAG when:</p>
<ul>
<li>The information needed to answer a query or complete a task isn't readily available in your Salesforce CRM data or the LLM's general knowledge.</li>
<li>You need the LLM to refer to specific documents, knowledge articles, external databases, or real-time information.</li>
<li>You want the LLM's response to be grounded in up-to-date or less common information.</li>
<li>You need to provide the LLM with context that is too lengthy to include directly in the prompt.
Explore more in the article, <a href="https://www.salesforce.com/agentforce/what-is-rag/">What Is Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)?</a>, and the Trailhead module, <a href="https://trailhead.salesforce.com/content/learn/modules/retrieval-augmented-generation-quick-look">Retrieval Augmented Generation: Quick Look</a></li>
</ul>

<h3>Snapshot Data</h3>
<p>CRM snapshot data refers to a historical record of your data at a specific point in time. Instead of showing the current, live state of your CRM records, a snapshot captures the data as it existed on a particular date and time. It’s like taking a photograph of your CRM data. Even if the actual records change later, the snapshot remains as a record of how things were at that moment.</p>
<p>Exploring with limited, targeted snapshots offers initial insights in rare cases, but it's generally inefficient and costly. The best approach is to understand the task, identify the core data requirements, and design prompts and data retrieval mechanisms that are precise and focused. Consider:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Snapshot data is often overwhelming.</strong> CRM snapshots often contain a vast amount of information across various entities, such as contacts, accounts, opportunities, activities, etc. The sheer volume makes it difficult for an LLM to process effectively and identify the truly relevant pieces.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Token inefficiency:</strong> Feeding large amounts of snapshot data consumes a significant number of tokens. This directly impacts the cost of using the LLM and leads to less efficient processing.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Potential for irrelevance.</strong> A large snapshot is likely to contain data points that are completely irrelevant to the specific prompt and task at hand. This noise dilutes important information and confuses the LLM.
However, there are limited scenarios where a carefully selected portion of snapshot data is a good starting point for exploration:</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Initial exploration with caution:</strong> If you have absolutely no prior knowledge of what data is relevant for a specific, novel prompting task, providing a small, targeted snapshot related to the core entity of the prompt offers some initial clues. For example, if the prompt is about a specific customer, a limited snapshot of that customer's recent activity helps to spark ideas.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Identifying relationships:</strong> In some complex scenarios, a broader snapshot initially helps the LLM identify relationships between different data points you weren't explicitly aware of. However, this needs to lead to more focused data retrieval quickly.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h3>Strategies for Data Placement within Prompts</h3>
<p>There are a few different options to consider for where to insert data for grounding in a prompt. Experiment with different placements to find what works best for your use case.</p>
<p><strong>Before instructions</strong></p>
<p>Place the grounding data at the very beginning of the prompt or immediately before the core instructions, using a distinct section header.</p>
<p><strong>Benefits</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Immediately provides the LLM with the necessary background data before it even reads the task. This helps it interpret the instructions with the correct lens and understand the specific situation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Keeps the core instructions distinct from the data, potentially making the prompt easier for both humans and the LLM to parse.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Allows the LLM to process the data first to influence how it approaches the subsequent instructions.
<strong>Drawbacks</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If the data is very long or contains irrelevant information, it tends to dilute the focus on the actual instructions</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Upfront instructions help the LLM immediately understand the task. Putting data first often makes it harder to immediately see what needs to be done.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Direct links between specific pieces of data and related instructions are less obvious if they are separated.
<strong>Inline with instructions</strong></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Embed the grounding data directly within the instructions where it becomes relevant. This involves using placeholders or directly inserting specific values.</p>
<p><strong>Benefits</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Clearly links the data to its specific instruction, making the relationship unambiguous.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The LLM considers that exact data when it's needed for a particular step or sub-task.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Avoids the need for a separate context section if the data is tightly integrated into the instructions.
<strong>Drawbacks</strong></p>
</li>
<li>

</li>
<li>
<p>Requires careful formatting and potentially delimiters to help the LLM correctly distinguish between instructions and data.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If the grounding data needs to be updated, you have to change it in multiple places within the instructions.
**After Instructions **</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Present the core instructions first, followed by a separate section containing the grounding data that the LLM needs to apply or reference to complete the task. We recommend placing larger, less immediately used datasets, like extensive lists or detailed JSON, at the very end of your prompt.</p>
<p><strong>Benefits</strong></p>

</li>
<li>
<p>Maintains a distinct separation between what to do and what to do it with.</p>
</li>
<li>

## Data for Grounding

Data provides the LLM with relevant, specific, and timely information from your Salesforce environment or external sources. This enables it to generate accurate, personalized, and contextually appropriate outputs that go beyond its general training data. This bridges the gap between the LLM's broad knowledge and the specific realities of your Salesforce world. Data for grounding includes:

- CRM data
- Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
- External data such as web search, user input, call transcript, or inbound email
While LLMs are incredibly knowledgeable, they don't inherently know *your* customers, *your* processes, or *your* data within Salesforce. Grounding is essential because it provides:

- **Relevance**. Without grounding, the LLM often produces generic outputs that don't quite fit your specific Salesforce context.
- **Personalization.** Providing specific details like customer names, account history, or case information enables the LLM to tailor its responses to feel genuinely relevant and impactful.
- **Accuracy.** When the LLM uses your verified Salesforce data, the output is more likely to be accurate and reliable for your specific business needs. Relying solely on the LLM's general knowledge often leads to inaccuracies or hallucinations.
- **Context.** Grounding provides the essential context the LLM needs to understand the nuances of your request. For example, knowing the stage of an Opportunity or the priority of a Case helps the LLM generate more helpful and targeted responses.

- Remember that the amount of data you add to a prompt is included in the overall token count, which potentially affects the cost of using the LLM.
- Larger amounts of data tend to also increase the processing time for the LLM.
The more specific and high quality your data is, the more specific and high quality the output is. For use cases where recency matters, like summarizing recent sales performance or open support tickets, provide the most up-to-date information available. If your data is outdated, inaccurate, or incomplete, the LLM output often reflects those flaws. The concept of garbage in, garbage out applies here.

- If the data has specific attributes or nuances that aren’t immediately obvious, briefly describe them to the LLM beforehand.
- For complex data structures, providing the LLM with a clear example of the data and the desired output often significantly improves its understanding and the accuracy of its response.
Include specific field-level data, like names, roles, and company names, to make the LLM's output feel more tailored and personalized to the individuals involved. Use Salesforce merge syntax, `{!object.field}`, to dynamically pull this information.

Explore RAG for information that lives outside of your immediate Salesforce records, like knowledge base articles, internal documents, or even external websites.

### Salesforce CRM Data

Salesforce objects, fields, and flows are usually the primary sources of grounding data within Salesforce. These include data stored in standard and custom objects, including:

- **Account:** Company information, industry, relationships, recent activities.
- **Contact:** Customer details, roles, communication preferences.
- **Opportunity:** Sales deals, stages, amounts, close dates, related products.
- **Case:** Customer support issues, status, history, resolutions.
- **Lead:** Potential customer information, engagement details.
- **Task and Event:** Activities, meetings, follow-up actions.
- **Custom Objects:** Data specific to your organization's unique processes and needs.
Other Salesforce data is found in:

- **User information:** Details about the current Salesforce user, such as name, role, and company.
- **Organization Information:** Details about your Salesforce instance.
- **Salesforce Flow outputs:** Structured data generated by automated processes within Salesforce.
- **Chat transcripts:** Records of conversations with customers through chat.
- **Email content :** The body and details of emails exchanged with customers.
- **Salesforce files:** Documents, attachments, and content stored within Salesforce Files that are relevant to the task.
- **Knowledge base articles (Salesforce Knowledge):** Information and solutions documented for customer service or internal use.
Create dynamic and interactive prompts by including user inputs. This means designing prompt templates that adapt to the specific information a user provides at runtime, making your LLM interactions more personalized and relevant. Other than Salesforce records and data, users are also able to input free text, image files, and lists of file inputs.

### External Knowledge with RAG

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) is a natural language processing technique that combines retrieval-based and generative models. RAG provides an LLM domain-specific knowledge retrieved from sources *outside* of its original training data and the immediate prompt context. Through **Data Cloud,** companies provide access to their most relevant and current proprietary data to make LLM-generated responses more accurate and tailored to their exact needs.

Use RAG when:

- The information needed to answer a query or complete a task isn't readily available in your Salesforce CRM data or the LLM's general knowledge.
- You need the LLM to refer to specific documents, knowledge articles, external databases, or real-time information.
- You want the LLM's response to be grounded in up-to-date or less common information.
- You need to provide the LLM with context that is too lengthy to include directly in the prompt.

### Snapshot Data

CRM snapshot data refers to a historical record of your data at a specific point in time. Instead of showing the current, live state of your CRM records, a snapshot captures the data as it existed on a particular date and time. It’s like taking a photograph of your CRM data. Even if the actual records change later, the snapshot remains as a record of how things were at that moment.

Exploring with limited, targeted snapshots offers initial insights in rare cases, but it's generally inefficient and costly. The best approach is to understand the task, identify the core data requirements, and design prompts and data retrieval mechanisms that are precise and focused. Consider:

- **Snapshot data is often overwhelming.** CRM snapshots often contain a vast amount of information across various entities, such as contacts, accounts, opportunities, activities, etc. The sheer volume makes it difficult for an LLM to process effectively and identify the truly relevant pieces.
- **Token inefficiency:** Feeding large amounts of snapshot data consumes a significant number of tokens. This directly impacts the cost of using the LLM and leads to less efficient processing.
- **Potential for irrelevance.** A large snapshot is likely to contain data points that are completely irrelevant to the specific prompt and task at hand. This noise dilutes important information and confuses the LLM.
However, there are limited scenarios where a carefully selected portion of snapshot data is a good starting point for exploration:

- **Initial exploration with caution:** If you have absolutely no prior knowledge of what data is relevant for a specific, novel prompting task, providing a small, targeted snapshot related to the core entity of the prompt offers some initial clues. For example, if the prompt is about a specific customer, a limited snapshot of that customer's recent activity helps to spark ideas.
- **Identifying relationships:** In some complex scenarios, a broader snapshot initially helps the LLM identify relationships between different data points you weren't explicitly aware of. However, this needs to lead to more focused data retrieval quickly.

### Strategies for Data Placement within Prompts

There are a few different options to consider for where to insert data for grounding in a prompt. Experiment with different placements to find what works best for your use case.

**Before instructions**

Place the grounding data at the very beginning of the prompt or immediately before the core instructions, using a distinct section header.

**Benefits**

- Immediately provides the LLM with the necessary background data before it even reads the task. This helps it interpret the instructions with the correct lens and understand the specific situation.
- Keeps the core instructions distinct from the data, potentially making the prompt easier for both humans and the LLM to parse.
- Allows the LLM to process the data first to influence how it approaches the subsequent instructions.
**Drawbacks**

- If the data is very long or contains irrelevant information, it tends to dilute the focus on the actual instructions
- Upfront instructions help the LLM immediately understand the task. Putting data first often makes it harder to immediately see what needs to be done.
- Direct links between specific pieces of data and related instructions are less obvious if they are separated.
**Inline with instructions**

Embed the grounding data directly within the instructions where it becomes relevant. This involves using placeholders or directly inserting specific values.

**Benefits**

- Clearly links the data to its specific instruction, making the relationship unambiguous.
- The LLM considers that exact data when it's needed for a particular step or sub-task.
- Avoids the need for a separate context section if the data is tightly integrated into the instructions.
**Drawbacks**

- Instructions are harder to read and follow, especially if there's a lot of embedded data. if the data breaks up the flow of the instructions too much, it confuses the LLM.
- Requires careful formatting and potentially delimiters to help the LLM correctly distinguish between instructions and data.
- If the grounding data needs to be updated, you have to change it in multiple places within the instructions.
**After Instructions **

Present the core instructions first, followed by a separate section containing the grounding data that the LLM needs to apply or reference to complete the task. We recommend placing larger, less immediately used datasets, like extensive lists or detailed JSON, at the very end of your prompt.

**Benefits**

- Helps the LLM clearly understand the task before being presented with the data to operate on.
- Maintains a distinct separation between what to do and what to do it with.
- Keeps your core instructions clean and easier to modify.
- Makes it easier to manage and update the grounding data as a separate block.
**Drawbacks**

- The LLM won’t have the specific context while initially processing the instructions. This potentially leads to a less nuanced understanding of task requirements.
- Requires explicit links for the LLM to find or use the data effectively.
