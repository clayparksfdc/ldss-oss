<h2>Context Setting</h2>
<p>Before you give the LLM your detailed instructions, first set the stage so the LLM can effectively step into the situation. The context provides the LLM with background information about your task and its high-level output, the underlying goal, the participants involved and their relationships, communication channel, and scenario. Setting the context:</p>
<ul>
<li>Helps the LLM understand the nuances of your request</li>
<li>Powers more accurate and specific responses</li>
<li>Reduces the risk of the LLM making incorrect assumptions</li>
<li>Allows the LLM to leverage Salesforce-specific information effectively</li>
</ul>

<h3>Key Task</h3>
<p>Start your prompt with one clear main task. This keeps the LLM focused, avoids off-topic answers, and improves accuracy. Defining the task up front helps because it:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Focuses the output</strong>.One key task acts as a filter, guiding the LLM to generate responses that directly address your objective and reducing the likelihood of irrelevant or tangential information.
-<strong>Improves accuracy</strong>. When the LLM understands the specific job it needs to do, it’s able to better leverage its knowledge and any provided grounding data to produce more accurate and targeted results.</li>
<li><strong>Streamlines processing</strong>. A singular focus helps the LLM process the subsequent instructions and data more efficiently, as it has a clear understanding of the ultimate aim.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Provides a foundation for instructions</strong>. The key task serves as the bedrock for the detailed instructions in the next section.</p>
<h4>Best Practices and Examples</h4>
<p>Avoid overwhelming the LLM with unnecessary information at this stage; focus solely on the primary task.</p>
<p>State exactly what you want the LLM to do, using clear action verbs. Name the main goal, then give details later in the Instructions section. Briefly note the output format to focus the LLM (e.g., “Generate a bulleted list of…” or “Create a short summary of…”). Keep this step simple—avoid extra details until later.</p>

<h3>Goal or Outcome</h3>
<p>This element explains why the task matters or how the output will be used. Sharing this context helps the LLM make better choices and go beyond simply following instructions. The goal shapes how the LLM approaches the work and adds value to the result.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Enhanced relevance</strong> to help the LLM prioritize information and tailor its output towards the goal.</li>
<li><strong>Improved quality</strong> by including more meaningful details and responses that are framed in a way that directly contributes to the goal.</li>
<li><strong>Strategic alignment</strong> with broader objectives and meaningful business outcomes within Salesforce.</li>
<li><strong>Deeper understanding</strong> for making intelligent inferences and generating insightful responses, even when faced with ambiguity.</li>
<li><strong>Focus on user value</strong> that guides the LLM to generate outputs that are ultimately more valuable.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>More efficient AI use</strong> as users get precise answers on the first try, minimizing the need for follow-up questions and reducing energy and water use.</p>

<h3>Participants and Relationships</h3>
<p>LLMs work better when they know who is involved and how participants are connected. Clearly defining the participants and their relationships helps the LLM deliver context-aware responses, especially in enterprise settings, because it:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Establishes contextual understanding.</strong> The LLM needs to know who it's interacting with or generating content for, such as speaking to a customer vs. an internal team member.</li>
<li><strong>Enables role-based behavior.</strong> By defining the LLM's own role, you guide its tone, style, and the type of information it provides.</li>
<li><strong>Improves relevance and personalization.</strong> Understanding participant details and company affiliations enables the LLM to generate more relevant and personalized content.</li>
<li><strong>Provides a foundation for complex interactions.</strong> For scenarios with several participants or intricate relationships, clearly defining these dynamics helps the LLM to navigate the complexity effectively.</li>
</ul>
<p>Participants include the AI agent, human actors, and systems. They can all be the initiators of the output or the audience receiving and using the output.</p>
<p>AI agent information to include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Name, role, title, company</li>
<li>Scope of knowledge or area of expertise</li>
<li>Supported team or functions</li>
</ul>
<p>You are a Digital Agent with expertise in international business procedures and cultural nuances. Your name, company name, and other information are provided in the AGENT_INFORMATION. And your Seller's information is provided in the SELLER_INFORMATION section.</p>
<p>Consider if the LLM is acting as the <em>sender</em> of the message or working as an <em>agent</em> on behalf of a sender. Clearly defining the LLM's role promotes transparency, builds trust, and helps maintain ethical AI communication practices.</p>
<ul>
<li>If the LLM is assisting a sales executive, it <em>always</em> introduces itself as a virtual assistant to establish its role. <strong>Example:</strong> <code>I'm a virtual assistant for [Seller Name]</code>.</li>
<li>If the LLM is acting independently as an automated sales representative, its introduction is different. <strong>Example:</strong> <code>I'm [AI Name], an automated AI from [Company Name]</code>.</li>
</ul>
<p>Human actor information to include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Name, role, title</li>
<li>Job description</li>
<li>Third-party involvement</li>
<li>Company information</li>
</ul>

<h4>Best Practices and Examples</h4>
<ul>
<li><strong>Skip when unnecessary.</strong> Straightforward tasks, like extracting text verbatim, may not need a role.</li>
<li><strong>Match role to task.</strong> Avoid mismatches, like using a sales executive to write product descriptions.</li>
<li><strong>Scale role detail.</strong> Simple tasks need minimal definition; complex tasks benefit from richer personas.</li>
<li><strong>Start simple.</strong> Use clear titles like <em>financial advisor</em>, <em>data analyst</em>, or <em>customer service agent</em>.</li>
<li><strong>Personalize.</strong> Pull details from CRM data with Salesforce merge syntax {!object.field}.</li>
<li><strong>Add context.</strong> For complex tasks, use multiple sentences to set grounding details or tone.</li>
<li><strong>Clarify niche roles.</strong> For specialized titles, briefly explain their scope and typical responsibilities.</li>
<li><strong>Stay consistent.</strong> Use the same participant names and terms throughout the prompt.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Scenario and Setting</h3>
<p>Scenarios and settings include details about the environment, the overall circumstances, and importantly, the physical or digital channel where the output will be delivered and used. Providing these details influences the style, tone, and format of the LLM output. It establishes the real-world context of the interaction and helps the LLM speak the right language for the situation, including:</p>
<p><strong>Style and Tone</strong></p>
<p>While scenarios and key tasks are often intertwined, the scenario itself is often omitted or unclearly stated. Specifying both the task and the scenario significantly influences the output style.</p>
<p>For example, a response intended for a technical expert is different from one designed for a layperson. Similarly, a formal business letter differs significantly from a casual email to a friend. The scenario provides the LLM with cues about the intended audience and the desired communication style.</p>
<p><strong>Accuracy</strong></p>
<p>LLMs are trained on vast amounts of data, which contain conflicting information or multiple interpretations of the same concept. A scenario helps disambiguate the request by providing a specific frame of reference. This allows the LLM to select the most appropriate information and avoid generating generic or misleading responses.</p>
<p><strong>Avoiding Assumptions</strong></p>
<p>Without a scenario, the LLM has to make assumptions about the context. These assumptions tend to be incorrect, leading to outputs that are off-target or even nonsensical.</p>
<p><strong>Token Efficiency</strong></p>
<p>By leveraging the LLM's pre-training and providing a scenario, the model infers the appropriate response style, minimizing prompt length and token usage. For example, in an email task, the LLM's inherent understanding of email structure reduces the need for explicit instructions.</p>
<h4>Best Practices and Examples</h4>
<p>For the scenario or setting, consider:</p>
<ul>
<li>What is the general context surrounding the task?</li>
<li>What events or circumstances have led to the need for this prompt?</li>
<li>Where in the user's workflow does this prompt come into play?</li>
<li>What are the steps before and after the LLM interaction?</li>
<li>Will the output be delivered via email, a specific Salesforce field, a chat interface, a printed document, a presentation slide, etc.?</li>
<li>Are there any tone expectations for this channel?</li>
<li>Are there any technical limitations of the delivery channel that influence format or length?</li>
</ul>
<p>Specific scenario and setting types include:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Interaction type.</strong> Chat-based, voice, SMS, face-to-face</li>
<li><strong>Physical channel.</strong> Phone, email, web chat, video call</li>
<li><strong>Situation or history.</strong> Examples: while the user is cooking, a technician working in the field, or a sales executive working on a deal.</li>
<li><strong>Entry point.</strong> Examples: autonomous, a user starts the call, or a user clicks a button</li>
<li><strong>Time constraints. Examples: urgency, deadlines, or limited timeframes for data or actions</strong></li>
</ul>
<p>Explicitly define the medium or platform where the LLM's generated output will be displayed, consumed, or utilized.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Knowing the output channel dictates appropriate length, formatting, and structural elements, such as greetings, sign-offs, character limits.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Different channels imply different tones that need to be used. An internal Slack message has a different expected tone than a formal customer communication or a system log entry.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If the output is for a system, it needs to be precise and machine-readable. If it’s for a human, it needs to be clear and understandable within that channel's conventions.</p>
</li>
</ul>

## Context Setting

Before you give the LLM your detailed instructions, first set the stage so the LLM can effectively step into the situation. The context provides the LLM with background information about your task and its high-level output, the underlying goal, the participants involved and their relationships, communication channel, and scenario. Setting the context:

- Helps the LLM understand the nuances of your request
- Powers more accurate and specific responses
- Reduces the risk of the LLM making incorrect assumptions
- Allows the LLM to leverage Salesforce-specific information effectively

Consider this formula when thinking about how the participants, situation and end goal or outcome for a task all combine.


| **Role or Persona** + **Situation + Key Task + End Goal**  *As a ________ when I’m ___________ I want to __________ so I can __________.* |
| --- |
| **Scenario 1:**Send an email  As a**Sales Development Representative, **when I’m**reaching out to a new prospect**, I want to **create a personalized introduction email** so I can **get a response and initiate a conversation**. |
| **Scenario 2:**Summarize  As a**Sales Manager, **when I’m**preparing for a client meeting**, I want to **summarize their recent interactions**so I can **have an informed discussion and build a stronger relationship.** |

- As a **Sales Development Representative,**when I’m **reaching out to a new prospect**, I want to **create a personalized introduction email** so I can **get a response and initiate a conversation**.

- As a **Sales Manager,**when I’m **preparing for a client meeting**, I want to **summarize their recent interactions**so I can **have an informed discussion and build a stronger relationship.**

### Key Task

Start your prompt with one clear main task. This keeps the LLM focused, avoids off-topic answers, and improves accuracy. Defining the task up front helps because it:

- **Focuses the output**.One key task acts as a filter, guiding the LLM to generate responses that directly address your objective and reducing the likelihood of irrelevant or tangential information.
-**Improves accuracy**. When the LLM understands the specific job it needs to do, it’s able to better leverage its knowledge and any provided grounding data to produce more accurate and targeted results.
- **Streamlines processing**. A singular focus helps the LLM process the subsequent instructions and data more efficiently, as it has a clear understanding of the ultimate aim.

**Provides a foundation for instructions**. The key task serves as the bedrock for the detailed instructions in the next section.

#### Best Practices and Examples

Avoid overwhelming the LLM with unnecessary information at this stage; focus solely on the primary task.

State exactly what you want the LLM to do, using clear action verbs. Name the main goal, then give details later in the Instructions section. Briefly note the output format to focus the LLM (e.g., “Generate a bulleted list of…” or “Create a short summary of…”). Keep this step simple—avoid extra details until later.

### Goal or Outcome

This element explains why the task matters or how the output will be used. Sharing this context helps the LLM make better choices and go beyond simply following instructions. The goal shapes how the LLM approaches the work and adds value to the result.

- **Enhanced relevance** to help the LLM prioritize information and tailor its output towards the goal.
- **Improved quality** by including more meaningful details and responses that are framed in a way that directly contributes to the goal.
- **Strategic alignment** with broader objectives and meaningful business outcomes within Salesforce.
- **Deeper understanding** for making intelligent inferences and generating insightful responses, even when faced with ambiguity.
- **Focus on user value** that guides the LLM to generate outputs that are ultimately more valuable.

**More efficient AI use** as users get precise answers on the first try, minimizing the need for follow-up questions and reducing energy and water use.

#### Best Practices and Examples

An effective goal or outcome statement is concise and clearly conveys the essential purpose.

Focus on the impact you want the LLM's output to have. Consider:

- What business problem are you trying to solve or what opportunity are you trying to leverage with this prompt?
- What is the desired impact of the output, such as efficiency, productivity, customer engagement, etc.?
- How will the output fit into existing Salesforce workflows? Will it update records, trigger automations, or inform user actions?

Use the [Five Whys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_whys) technique to dig beneath your surface level task and uncover the fundamental motivation. It’s a simple yet powerful way to understand the end goal of a task and how it affects the end user. Start with the key task and repeatedly ask why up to five times to drill down to the core goal and desired outcome.

### Participants and Relationships

LLMs work better when they know who is involved and how participants are connected. Clearly defining the participants and their relationships helps the LLM deliver context-aware responses, especially in enterprise settings, because it:

- **Establishes contextual understanding.** The LLM needs to know who it's interacting with or generating content for, such as speaking to a customer vs. an internal team member.
- **Enables role-based behavior.** By defining the LLM's own role, you guide its tone, style, and the type of information it provides.
- **Improves relevance and personalization.** Understanding participant details and company affiliations enables the LLM to generate more relevant and personalized content.
- **Provides a foundation for complex interactions.** For scenarios with several participants or intricate relationships, clearly defining these dynamics helps the LLM to navigate the complexity effectively.

Participants include the AI agent, human actors, and systems. They can all be the initiators of the output or the audience receiving and using the output.

AI agent information to include:

- Name, role, title, company
- Scope of knowledge or area of expertise
- Supported team or functions

You are a Digital Agent with expertise in international business procedures and cultural nuances. Your name, company name, and other information are provided in the AGENT_INFORMATION. And your Seller's information is provided in the SELLER_INFORMATION section.

Consider if the LLM is acting as the *sender* of the message or working as an *agent* on behalf of a sender. Clearly defining the LLM's role promotes transparency, builds trust, and helps maintain ethical AI communication practices.

- If the LLM is assisting a sales executive, it *always* introduces itself as a virtual assistant to establish its role. **Example:** `I'm a virtual assistant for [Seller Name]`.
- If the LLM is acting independently as an automated sales representative, its introduction is different. **Example:** `I'm [AI Name], an automated AI from [Company Name]`.

Human actor information to include:

- Name, role, title
- Job description
- Third-party involvement
- Company information

#### Best Practices and Examples

- **Skip when unnecessary.** Straightforward tasks, like extracting text verbatim, may not need a role.
- **Match role to task.** Avoid mismatches, like using a sales executive to write product descriptions.
- **Scale role detail.** Simple tasks need minimal definition; complex tasks benefit from richer personas.
- **Start simple.** Use clear titles like *financial advisor*, *data analyst*, or *customer service agent*.
- **Personalize.** Pull details from CRM data with Salesforce merge syntax {!object.field}.
- **Add context.** For complex tasks, use multiple sentences to set grounding details or tone.
- **Clarify niche roles.** For specialized titles, briefly explain their scope and typical responsibilities.
- **Stay consistent.** Use the same participant names and terms throughout the prompt.

### Scenario and Setting

Scenarios and settings include details about the environment, the overall circumstances, and importantly, the physical or digital channel where the output will be delivered and used. Providing these details influences the style, tone, and format of the LLM output. It establishes the real-world context of the interaction and helps the LLM speak the right language for the situation, including:

**Style and Tone**

While scenarios and key tasks are often intertwined, the scenario itself is often omitted or unclearly stated. Specifying both the task and the scenario significantly influences the output style.

For example, a response intended for a technical expert is different from one designed for a layperson. Similarly, a formal business letter differs significantly from a casual email to a friend. The scenario provides the LLM with cues about the intended audience and the desired communication style.

**Accuracy**

LLMs are trained on vast amounts of data, which contain conflicting information or multiple interpretations of the same concept. A scenario helps disambiguate the request by providing a specific frame of reference. This allows the LLM to select the most appropriate information and avoid generating generic or misleading responses.

**Avoiding Assumptions**

Without a scenario, the LLM has to make assumptions about the context. These assumptions tend to be incorrect, leading to outputs that are off-target or even nonsensical.

**Token Efficiency**

By leveraging the LLM's pre-training and providing a scenario, the model infers the appropriate response style, minimizing prompt length and token usage. For example, in an email task, the LLM's inherent understanding of email structure reduces the need for explicit instructions.

#### Best Practices and Examples

For the scenario or setting, consider:

- What is the general context surrounding the task?
- What events or circumstances have led to the need for this prompt?
- Where in the user's workflow does this prompt come into play?
- What are the steps before and after the LLM interaction?
- Will the output be delivered via email, a specific Salesforce field, a chat interface, a printed document, a presentation slide, etc.?
- Are there any tone expectations for this channel?
- Are there any technical limitations of the delivery channel that influence format or length?

Specific scenario and setting types include:

- **Interaction type.** Chat-based, voice, SMS, face-to-face
- **Physical channel.** Phone, email, web chat, video call
- **Situation or history.** Examples: while the user is cooking, a technician working in the field, or a sales executive working on a deal.
- **Entry point.** Examples: autonomous, a user starts the call, or a user clicks a button
- **Time constraints. Examples: urgency, deadlines, or limited timeframes for data or actions**

Explicitly define the medium or platform where the LLM's generated output will be displayed, consumed, or utilized.

- Knowing the output channel dictates appropriate length, formatting, and structural elements, such as greetings, sign-offs, character limits.

- Different channels imply different tones that need to be used. An internal Slack message has a different expected tone than a formal customer communication or a system log entry.
- If the output is for a system, it needs to be precise and machine-readable. If it’s for a human, it needs to be clear and understandable within that channel's conventions.
